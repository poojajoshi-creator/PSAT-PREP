Skill 1

Ratios, Rates, Proportions, and Percentages

A ratio compares two quantities; a rate compares quantities with different units (miles per hour, dollars per item); a proportion sets two ratios equal so you can scale up or down. A percentage is just a ratio out of 100, and percent change is always calculated relative to the original value, not the new one.

What to watch for
  • When setting up a proportion, make sure matching units line up in the same position in each ratio.
  • Percent change = (new value − original value) ÷ original value × 100.
  • "X% of" a number means multiply; "increased by X%" means multiply by (1 + X%).
Common mistakes
  • Dividing by the new value instead of the original value when finding percent change.
  • Setting up a proportion with mismatched units on each side.
  • Confusing "percent of" with "percent increase/decrease" in a word problem.
Skill 2

One-Variable Data: Distributions and Measures of Center and Spread

Mean, median, and mode all describe the "center" of a data set, but they respond very differently to outliers. The mean shifts toward an extreme value; the median barely moves. Spread — range and standard deviation — describes how spread out the data is, which is a separate question from where its center sits.

What to watch for
  • An outlier pulls the mean toward it much more than it affects the median.
  • Adding the same constant to every value shifts the mean and median but leaves the spread unchanged.
  • Multiplying every value by a constant changes the spread too, not just the center.
Common mistakes
  • Assuming mean and median will be close together, even when a data set is skewed by an outlier.
  • Comparing two data sets using only center, while ignoring differences in spread.
  • Mixing up which transformation (adding vs. multiplying) actually changes the spread.
Skill 3

Two-Variable Data: Models and Scatterplots

A scatterplot shows the relationship between two variables; a line of best fit summarizes that relationship so you can predict one variable from the other. The strength of a relationship is about how tightly the points cluster around the line, not just whether the slope is positive or negative. A clear relationship in the data never by itself proves that one variable causes the other.

What to watch for
  • To predict a value from a line of best fit, just plug the given x-value into the equation.
  • Tightly clustered points mean a strong relationship; widely scattered points mean a weak one, regardless of slope direction.
  • Read the slope's units in context — it's a rate of change, not just a number.
Common mistakes
  • Treating a strong correlation as proof of cause and effect.
  • Extrapolating a model far beyond the range of the original data with too much confidence.
  • Confusing the strength of a relationship with the steepness of its slope.
Skill 4

Probability and Conditional Probability

Basic probability is favorable outcomes divided by total outcomes. Two-way frequency tables organize counts across two categories at once. Conditional probability ("given that...") narrows your denominator to only the relevant row or column of the table, rather than the table's full total.

What to watch for
  • For "given that" questions, the denominator is the total of just the specified group, not everyone in the table.
  • For "and," multiply probabilities (for independent events); for "or," add them and subtract any overlap.
  • Double-check which row or column a two-way table's labels actually refer to before reading off a count.
Common mistakes
  • Using the table's grand total as the denominator for a conditional probability question.
  • Forgetting to subtract the overlap when calculating the probability of "A or B."
  • Mixing up a row total with a column total when reading a two-way table.
Skill 5

Statistical Inference and Evaluating Claims

A sample statistic estimates a population parameter, and the margin of error defines a plausible range around that estimate. A larger, more randomly selected sample generally produces a smaller margin of error. Critically: only a randomized controlled experiment can support a cause-and-effect claim. An observational study, no matter how strong the pattern, can only support a claim about correlation.

What to watch for
  • A result is only generalizable to the population it was actually randomly sampled from — not to a broader group.
  • A margin of error defines a range (statistic ± margin); check whether a claimed value actually falls inside that range.
  • Look for the word "random assignment" — that's what allows a study to support a causal claim, not just a large sample.
Common mistakes
  • Concluding causation from an observational study that only shows correlation.
  • Generalizing survey results to a population that wasn't actually sampled.
  • Treating any difference between two statistics as meaningful without checking it against the margin of error.
