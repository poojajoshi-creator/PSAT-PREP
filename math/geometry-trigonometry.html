Skill 1

Area and Volume

Most area and volume questions combine a formula you already know with a shape that's built from simpler pieces — a rectangle with a triangle cut off the corner, or a cylinder sitting inside a word problem about filling a tank. The formula is rarely the hard part; tracking the units and the shape's actual boundaries is.

What to watch for
  • For a composite shape, break it into simpler shapes you know, then add or subtract their areas.
  • Make sure all measurements are in the same unit before plugging into a formula.
  • Double-check whether a given measurement is a radius or a diameter before using it.
Common mistakes
  • Using a diameter where the formula calls for a radius (or vice versa).
  • Adding a cut-out region's area instead of subtracting it.
  • Mixing units (feet and inches, for example) without converting first.
Skill 2

Lines, Angles, and Triangles

When a transversal crosses two parallel lines, several pairs of angles are automatically equal (corresponding and alternate interior angles). Inside any triangle, the three angles always sum to 180°, and an exterior angle always equals the sum of the two remote interior angles. Similar triangles have equal corresponding angles and proportional corresponding sides — the trick is correctly matching up which side corresponds to which.

What to watch for
  • With parallel lines, look for the angle pair pattern (corresponding, alternate interior) before assuming two angles are equal.
  • For similar triangles, match sides based on which angles are equal, not just which sides look closest in size on the page.
  • A straight line totals 180° and a full turn around a point totals 360° — useful for filling in missing angles.
Common mistakes
  • Assuming triangles are similar without checking that their angles actually correspond.
  • Mismatching corresponding sides when a triangle is rotated or flipped in the diagram.
  • Forgetting the exterior angle theorem and trying to resolve everything with the interior angle sum alone.
Skill 3

Right Triangles and Trigonometry

The Pythagorean theorem (a2 + b2 = c2) only applies to right triangles. Basic trig ratios — sine, cosine, and tangent — relate an angle to the ratio of two sides, using SOH-CAH-TOA: sine = opposite/hypotenuse, cosine = adjacent/hypotenuse, tangent = opposite/adjacent. Two special triangles, 45-45-90 and 30-60-90, have fixed side ratios that are often faster than computing trig values directly.

What to watch for
  • "Opposite" and "adjacent" depend on which angle you're using — relabel them each time you switch angles.
  • In a 45-45-90 triangle, the hypotenuse is a leg times √2. In a 30-60-90 triangle, the sides are in the ratio 1 : √3 : 2.
  • sin(θ) = cos(90° − θ) for any angle θ — useful for "complementary angle" questions.
Common mistakes
  • Mixing up which side is opposite versus adjacent for the angle in question.
  • Applying the Pythagorean theorem to a triangle that isn't actually a right triangle.
  • Forgetting the sine/cosine complementary angle relationship on questions designed to test it.
Skill 4

Circles

Arc length and sector area are always some fraction of the whole circle's circumference or area, based on the central angle out of 360°. The equation of a circle, (x − h)2 + (y − k)2 = r2, gives the center (h, k) directly — but watch the sign, since a "+3" inside the parentheses actually means h = −3. An inscribed angle is always half of the central angle that subtends the same arc.

What to watch for
  • Arc length = (central angle ÷ 360°) × circumference; sector area = (central angle ÷ 360°) × area.
  • In the circle equation, flip the sign inside each set of parentheses to find the actual center coordinate.
  • An inscribed angle is half its corresponding central angle — they are not equal.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting to divide by 360° when computing an arc length or sector area.
  • Reading the wrong sign for the center's coordinates from the circle equation.
  • Treating an inscribed angle as equal to, rather than half of, its central angle.
