Skill 1

Linear Equations in One Variable

This is the most basic algebra skill on the test: distribute, combine like terms, and isolate the variable, including equations that have the variable on both sides. The setup is usually the hard part — word problems often hide the equation inside a sentence about cost, distance, or time.

Whatever you do to one side of the equation, you must do to the other. That single rule covers nearly every step you'll take here.

What to watch for
  • When distributing a negative across parentheses, every term inside flips sign — not just the first one.
  • Combine like terms only after they're on the same side of the equation.
  • In word problems, write out the equation in words first, then translate piece by piece.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting to distribute a negative sign to every term inside parentheses.
  • Making an arithmetic sign error when moving a term across the equals sign.
  • Performing an operation on only one side of the equation.
Skill 2

Linear Functions and Equations in Two Variables

A linear equation in two variables (often written y = mx + b) describes a straight line. The slope (m) is the rate of change — how much y changes for every one-unit increase in x — and the y-intercept (b) is the starting value, where the line crosses the y-axis. In word problems, this usually maps to a per-unit rate and a flat starting amount.

What to watch for
  • Slope = rise over run: change in y divided by change in x between two points.
  • In a word problem, the flat fee or starting amount is the intercept; the per-unit rate is the slope.
  • A negative slope means the quantity is decreasing as x increases — check that the sign matches the scenario.
Common mistakes
  • Swapping rise and run when calculating slope from two points.
  • Mixing up which number in a word problem is the slope and which is the intercept.
  • Sign errors when a relationship is decreasing rather than increasing.
Skill 3

Systems of Two Linear Equations in Two Variables

A system of two linear equations asks: where do these two lines intersect? Substitution and elimination both get you there. Sometimes the question doesn't ask you to solve at all — it asks how many solutions the system has, which you can answer just by comparing slopes and intercepts: different slopes means exactly one solution, same slope with different intercepts means no solution (parallel lines), and same slope with the same intercept means infinitely many solutions (the same line).

What to watch for
  • For elimination, line up matching variables before adding or subtracting the equations.
  • For substitution, plug your expression into the other equation, not the one you started with.
  • "How many solutions" questions don't require solving — just compare slope and intercept.
Common mistakes
  • Sign errors when subtracting one full equation from another in elimination.
  • Substituting a value back into the same equation it came from, which proves nothing new.
  • Assuming every system has exactly one solution.
Skill 4

Linear Inequalities in One or Two Variables

Inequalities behave like equations with one critical exception: multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number flips the direction of the inequality sign. In two variables, an inequality describes a whole region of the coordinate plane rather than a single line — useful for word problems involving limits like "at least" or "no more than."

What to watch for
  • Multiplying or dividing by a negative number flips the inequality sign — adding or subtracting never does.
  • "At least" means ≥, "at most" or "no more than" means ≤. Translate the phrase before writing the inequality.
  • For two-variable inequalities, test a single point to confirm which side of the line is the solution region.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting to flip the inequality sign after multiplying or dividing by a negative number.
  • Mixing up "at least" and "at most" when translating word problems.
  • Shading or selecting the wrong side of a boundary line in a two-variable inequality.
