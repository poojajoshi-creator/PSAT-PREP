Skill 1

Nonlinear Functions

This covers quadratic, exponential, polynomial, and radical functions — how their equations relate to their graphs. A quadratic in vertex form, y = a(x − h)2 + k, hands you the vertex (h, k) directly. An exponential function, y = a · bx, grows if the base b is greater than 1 and decays if b is between 0 and 1.

What to watch for
  • In vertex form a(x − h)2 + k, the vertex is (h, k) — watch the sign inside the parentheses.
  • A negative leading coefficient flips a parabola to open downward, giving it a maximum instead of a minimum.
  • For exponentials, check whether the base is above or below 1 before assuming growth or decay.
Common mistakes
  • Misreading the sign of h in vertex form (the vertex's x-value is the opposite of what's written inside the parentheses).
  • Treating an exponential growth word problem as if it were linear.
  • Forgetting that a negative leading coefficient changes a minimum to a maximum.
Skill 2

Nonlinear Equations in One Variable

Solving a quadratic usually means factoring first, and falling back on the quadratic formula only when factoring doesn't work cleanly. Equations involving square roots or rational expressions need an extra step: after solving, plug each answer back into the original equation, since squaring both sides (or multiplying out a denominator) can introduce a solution that doesn't actually work.

What to watch for
  • Try factoring before reaching for the quadratic formula — it's usually faster when the numbers are clean.
  • Taking a square root of both sides produces two solutions: positive and negative.
  • Always check radical and rational-expression solutions in the original equation for extraneous roots.
Common mistakes
  • Forgetting the negative root when taking a square root of both sides.
  • Accepting an extraneous solution that doesn't actually satisfy the original equation.
  • Sign errors while factoring, especially with negative constant terms.
Skill 3

Systems with a Nonlinear Equation

These systems usually pair one linear equation with one quadratic (or other nonlinear) equation — geometrically, a line and a curve. Substituting the linear expression into the nonlinear equation turns the system into a single equation you already know how to solve. Because a line can cross a parabola zero, one (tangent), or two times, these systems don't always have exactly one solution.

What to watch for
  • Substitute the linear equation into the nonlinear one, then solve the resulting equation for x.
  • Each valid x-value needs its own y-value — don't stop after finding just one solution.
  • Check final answers in both original equations, not just the one you substituted into.
Common mistakes
  • Reporting only one solution when the system actually has two.
  • Algebra slips during substitution, especially with squared terms.
  • Assuming a line and a parabola must intersect, when the discriminant shows they don't.
Skill 4

Equivalent Expressions

These questions ask you to rewrite an expression in a different but mathematically identical form — through factoring, expanding, simplifying a rational expression, or applying exponent rules. Recognizing common patterns, like the difference of squares (a2 − b2 = (a − b)(a + b)), saves real time.

What to watch for
  • Factor the numerator and denominator of a rational expression fully before canceling anything.
  • You can only cancel factors that are multiplied, never terms that are added or subtracted.
  • Exponent rules: multiplying same-base powers adds exponents; raising a power to a power multiplies them.
Common mistakes
  • Canceling a term that's part of a sum rather than a true multiplied factor.
  • Adding exponents when the bases aren't actually the same.
  • Sign errors when factoring out a negative number from an expression.
