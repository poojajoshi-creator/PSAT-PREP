Skill 1

Central Ideas and Details

The passage is short — usually under 150 words — so it's built around one main point, with everything else there to support it. This skill asks you to separate the point from the proof: what is the passage actually arguing or reporting, versus what's just an example or piece of supporting detail?

The right answer is usually a sentence that could stand in as a one-line summary of the whole passage — not a fact pulled from just one part of it.

What to watch for
  • The first and last sentence of a short passage often carry the most weight — that's usually where the thesis sits.
  • Emphasis words like "but," "the key," or "ultimately" often flag the real point, especially mid-passage.
  • Ask "could this sentence summarize the whole thing?" before picking it.
Common mistakes
  • Picking an answer that's true but only covers part of the passage — a detail dressed up as the main idea.
  • Picking an answer that's too broad — technically related, but goes beyond what the passage actually says.
  • Confusing the topic ("what it's about") with the main idea ("what it's saying about that topic").
Skill 2

Inferences

An inference question asks what the passage implies but never states directly. The answer still has to be earned from the text — you're taking one small, logical step beyond what's written, not guessing or filling in with what you already know about the topic.

Look for the question stem to use words like "suggests," "implies," or "most likely believes." That's your signal that the answer won't be quoted directly — it has to be assembled from what's given.

What to watch for
  • The correct inference is the smallest reasonable step from the text — not the most dramatic one.
  • If you can point to the exact sentence(s) that support it, you're on the right track.
  • Treat each answer choice as a claim you have to prove using only the passage.
Common mistakes
  • Using outside knowledge about the topic instead of sticking to what's on the page.
  • Picking an answer with absolute language ("always," "never," "proves") when the passage only supports something likely or partial.
  • Overthinking it — the test rewards the obvious next step, not the clever one.
Skill 3

Command of Evidence

These questions give you a claim and ask which piece of evidence best supports it — either a quote/detail from a text, or a data point from a table, bar graph, or line graph. The skill is matching evidence to a specific claim, not just finding evidence that's generally on-topic.

For graph-based versions: read the title, axis labels, and units before you even look at the answer choices. A surprising number of wrong answers are wrong simply because they misread what the graph is measuring.

What to watch for
  • Reread the claim in the question stem first, then test each option only against that exact claim.
  • For graphs, identify the units and direction (increasing/decreasing) before scanning the choices.
  • The best evidence directly supports the claim — it doesn't just relate to the same general topic.
Common mistakes
  • Choosing evidence that's interesting or related but doesn't actually prove the specific claim asked about.
  • Misreading a graph's axis or units under time pressure.
  • Going for the most "extreme-looking" data point instead of the one that matches the claim's wording.
