Skill 1

Rhetorical Synthesis

You're given a set of bullet-point notes about a topic and a specific goal — "emphasize a similarity," "highlight the most surprising finding," "introduce the topic to an unfamiliar audience" — and asked which sentence best accomplishes that exact goal using only the given information.

Every answer choice will usually be a true statement based on the bullets. The test isn't checking whether you can spot a true sentence — it's checking whether you can match a sentence to the specific goal stated in the question.

What to watch for
  • Reread the stated goal slowly before scanning the bullets — "difference" vs. "similarity," "cause" vs. "effect" changes everything.
  • Use only the information given in the bullets; don't add outside knowledge even if you have it.
Common mistakes
  • Picking a true, well-written sentence that doesn't actually match the specific goal asked for.
  • Combining bullets in a way that isn't supported by how they're written.
Skill 2

Transitions

Choose the word or phrase that correctly connects two ideas: continuation ("furthermore," "similarly"), contrast ("however," "yet"), cause-and-effect ("therefore," "as a result"), or example ("for instance"). The skill is identifying the logical relationship first, then matching it to a category of transition — not picking whichever option sounds smoothest.

What to watch for
  • Cover the blank and describe the relationship between the two sentences in your own words first: agreement, contrast, cause, or example.
  • Watch for transitions that are close cousins but logically different — "however" (contrast) vs. "therefore" (cause/effect).
Common mistakes
  • Choosing a transition because it "sounds right" without checking the actual logical relationship.
  • Confusing addition words ("moreover") with contrast words ("nevertheless") when skimming quickly.
