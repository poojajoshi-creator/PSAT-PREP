Skill 1

Words in Context

You're given a sentence with a blank and asked to pick the word or phrase that best fits. This isn't a vocabulary quiz in the "memorize definitions" sense — it's about figuring out the exact shade of meaning the sentence needs, then finding the option that matches.

Many of the trickiest questions use words you already know, but in a less common secondary meaning, or pair an easy word with a tricky contrast structure around the blank.

What to watch for
  • Contrast and cause words near the blank ("however," "despite," "because," "although") tell you the direction the missing word has to go.
  • Check tone — is the sentence positive, negative, or neutral about its subject?
  • Try predicting your own word for the blank before reading the choices, then match the closest one.
Common mistakes
  • Picking the word you recognize fastest rather than the one that actually fits this specific sentence.
  • Missing a negation ("not," "rarely," "unlike") that flips the needed meaning.
  • Assuming the most common dictionary definition when the passage is using a less common sense of the word.
Skill 2

Text Structure and Purpose

These questions ask why the author organized the passage a certain way, or what job a specific sentence is doing — introducing an idea, complicating it, providing support, qualifying an earlier claim, or wrapping things up.

The key distinction is between what a sentence says and what it does. A sentence can state a fact while its real job in the passage is to set up a contrast with what comes next.

What to watch for
  • Map the passage's shape as you read: claim → complication → resolution, or problem → solution, or chronological order.
  • Transition words ("however," "for example," "as a result") are signposts for a sentence's function.
  • For "purpose of this sentence" questions, describe its job in your own words before checking the answers.
Common mistakes
  • Answering with what the sentence says instead of what role it plays in the passage's argument.
  • Missing a shift signaled by a small transition word and misreading the passage's overall direction.
Skill 3

Cross-Text Connections

You're given two short, related passages — often two writers with differing views on the same topic — and asked how they relate: do they agree, disagree, address different parts of the same question, or does one complicate the other?

What to watch for
  • Summarize each passage's claim separately, in your own words, before comparing them.
  • Look for whether the two passages are even addressing the exact same question — sometimes only one part overlaps.
Common mistakes
  • Mixing up which passage holds which view under time pressure.
  • Assuming full agreement or disagreement when the real relationship is partial overlap.
