Skill 1

Boundaries

Boundaries questions test whether you know how to correctly join or separate clauses using periods, semicolons, colons, commas, and dashes. The core move is recognizing independent clauses (complete sentences on their own) versus dependent clauses (incomplete on their own), and knowing which punctuation can legally connect which combination.

Two independent clauses can be joined by a period, a semicolon, or a comma + conjunction (and, but, so, or, yet, for, nor). A comma by itself is not strong enough — that's a comma splice, one of the single most common wrong answers on this test.

What to watch for
  • Mentally split the sentence at the punctuation mark and check: is each side a complete sentence on its own?
  • Colons and dashes can introduce an explanation or list, but only after a complete independent clause.
Common mistakes
  • Comma splices — joining two complete sentences with just a comma.
  • Treating a dependent clause (starting with "because," "although," "when") as if it were a complete sentence.
  • Adding unnecessary commas around information that's essential to the sentence's meaning.
Skill 2

Form, Structure, and Sense

This covers subject-verb agreement, consistent verb tense, clear pronoun reference, modifier placement, and parallel structure in lists. It's less about punctuation marks and more about whether the sentence's grammar actually holds together logically.

What to watch for
  • Find the true subject of the verb, even when it's separated by a long phrase ("The list of items on the shelf is/are...").
  • Check that every pronoun ("it," "they," "this") clearly points to one specific noun.
  • Scan for a consistent timeline — if the passage is in past tense, a sudden present-tense verb usually signals an error.
Common mistakes
  • Subject-verb agreement errors caused by a prepositional phrase sitting between subject and verb.
  • Verb tense that shifts without a logical reason to.
  • Modifiers placed so they describe the wrong word ("Walking to school, the rain soaked her backpack").
